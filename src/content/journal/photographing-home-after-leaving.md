---
title: "Photographing Home After Leaving It"
date: 2026-02-28
excerpt: "I left Kabul under direct threat. Now I photograph cities from the outside — as someone who knows what it means to lose access to the streets you used to walk without thinking."

There is a particular kind of attention you develop when a city is no longer available to you.

I lived in Kabul for years. I walked its streets without a plan, photographing the markets, the intersections, the quiet moments between buildings. I knew which alley would catch the afternoon light. I knew where the bread sellers set up before dawn. I knew the city the way you know a place you do not think about losing.

Then I had to leave.

The circumstances are not the subject of this post. What matters here is what happened to my relationship with photography after displacement.

When you lose a city, you start to see every city differently. You notice things that residents take for granted — the freedom of walking without calculation, the luxury of a routine, the quiet belonging that comes from knowing where you are. In Kabul, I photographed as an insider. In Boston, I photograph as someone who is learning to belong.

Both perspectives produce honest work. But they are different kinds of honesty.

The Kabul photographs carry an intimacy that comes from years of accumulated presence. I did not need to earn access to those moments — I was already inside them. The market vendors knew me. The children were not performing for a stranger. The light was familiar.

The Boston photographs carry a different quality — the heightened attention of someone who is still mapping a new environment. I notice the commuter rail schedules, the demographic shifts between neighborhoods, the way people arrange themselves on park benches. I am reading the city with a trained eye and a newcomer's hunger.

What connects the two bodies of work is the same fundamental impulse: to pay attention to the everyday life of a city and to insist that it deserves to be seen.

Displacement sharpens that impulse. When you have had a city taken from you, you do not take the next one for granted. Every walk becomes a small act of reclamation. Every photograph becomes proof that you are here, that you are looking, that you have not stopped caring about the places where people live.

The Kabul Streets project — the photo book I am developing from five years of iPhone photography — is my way of returning to a city I cannot visit. The images are not a record of what Kabul looks like now. They are a record of what it looked like when I was there, when I could still walk its streets freely.

That is the paradox of documentary photography after displacement. You are always photographing in two directions — toward the place you are in and toward the place you carry with you.
