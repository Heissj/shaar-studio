title: "Before It Disappears: Photography as Heritage Conservation"
date: 2026-03-18
excerpt: "Some places exist on borrowed time. Photographing them is not nostalgia — it is an act of preservation, a record that says: this was here, this mattered, this was worth protecting."

---

In 2017, I stood at Mes-e Aynak, a Buddhist archaeological site south of Kabul that sits on top of one of the largest copper deposits in the world. The site dates back over 5,000 years. It contains stupas, monasteries, and artifacts that rewrite the history of Central Asian Buddhism. And it was slated for demolition to make way for mining.

I was there as part of a management planning effort — working with international teams to document what existed before the excavators arrived. But it was the camera, not the planning documents, that made me understand the urgency.

A photograph of a carved stone Buddha emerging from the earth after centuries tells a story that no heritage impact assessment can. It communicates value in a language that crosses borders, languages, and expertise levels. It says: look at this. This is irreplaceable.

Heritage conservation is, at its foundation, an argument about value. And photography is one of the most powerful ways to make that argument.

In Afghanistan, where decades of conflict have destroyed or damaged countless sites, documentation often becomes the conservation. The physical structure may not survive. The photograph endures. It becomes the archive, the evidence, the memory.

But this is not only about conflict zones. Every city contains heritage at risk. In Boston, I walk past buildings whose stories are being erased by development. In Kabul, entire neighborhoods that defined the city for generations have been replaced by concrete towers. The loss is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is quiet — a shopfront repainted, a courtyard walled off, a street pattern straightened.

Street photography, in this context, is a form of vernacular heritage documentation. You are recording not the monuments but the everyday fabric — the textures, the uses, the human presence that gives a place its character.

The lesson from Mes-e Aynak is simple and difficult: document what you love before it disappears. Because sometimes the photograph is all that will remain.
