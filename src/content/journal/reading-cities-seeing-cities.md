There is a way planners look at cities that most people never learn. You see a busy intersection and notice the pedestrian desire lines worn into the grass where the sidewalk should be. You see a vacant lot and read the zoning code that created it. You see a thriving block and understand the mix of uses, the building setbacks, the transit access that made it possible.

This training is a gift and a limitation.

The gift is pattern recognition. After years of working in urban development — in Afghanistan, where I helped coordinate national urban policy, and now in Boston, where I study urban informatics — I cannot walk through a city without reading its structure. I notice the width of streets, the placement of doorways, the rhythm of commerce. I see systems.

The limitation is that systems thinking can flatten experience. A city is not only its zoning map. It is the sound of a vendor calling out prices at dusk. It is the way a grandmother positions her chair to catch the last hour of sunlight. It is the child running between parked cars with a kite made from a plastic bag.

Photography pulls me back into those moments.

When I pick up the camera, the analytical eye does not disappear. I still frame shots with an awareness of spatial composition that comes from reading site plans. I still notice the relationship between built form and human behavior. But the camera demands something more: presence. It asks me to stop analyzing and start attending.

The best photographs I take happen in the gap between these two modes. The planner sees a narrow market alley and understands its function within the neighborhood economy. The photographer sees the light filtering through the awnings, the hands exchanging bread, the stillness of a man drinking tea while the city moves around him. The photograph holds both.

I think this is what SHAAR Studio is really about. SHAAR means city in Dari, and the name carries both meanings — the city as a system and the city as an experience. Every image I make is an attempt to honor that duality.
