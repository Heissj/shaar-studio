Every photographer eventually has the gear conversation. What camera do you shoot with? What lens? What settings? The assumption behind the question is that better equipment produces better photographs.

The Kabul Streets project — five years of documentary street photography — was shot entirely on an iPhone.

This was not an ideological choice. It was a practical one that became a creative philosophy.

In Kabul, carrying a professional camera changes your relationship with the street. A DSLR announces your presence. It creates distance. People see the equipment before they see you, and their behavior shifts — they pose, or they turn away, or they ask what you are doing. The camera becomes a barrier.

The phone is different. Everyone has a phone. Pulling one out on a busy street is invisible. You become just another person checking a message, and in that invisibility, the city continues being itself. The bread seller keeps working. The children keep playing. The old man keeps drinking his tea in the doorway. The moments remain unperformed.

There are trade-offs. The dynamic range is limited. Low light is challenging. You cannot control depth of field the way you can with a fast prime lens. But these constraints do something unexpected: they force you to work with what is actually in front of you rather than manufacturing a scene.

You learn to wait for the light instead of compensating for it. You move closer because you cannot zoom. You compose more carefully because you cannot crop your way out of a lazy frame. The limitations become a discipline.

There is also something philosophically aligned between phone photography and street photography. Both are about immediacy. Street photography is the art of responding to the unrepeatable moment. The phone — always in your pocket, always ready — matches that ethos. There is no bag to open, no lens to change, no settings to adjust. You see it, you raise the phone, you take the shot. The gap between seeing and capturing shrinks to almost nothing.

The lesson is one that photographers need to hear: the best tool is the one that gets out of the way. The best camera is the one that lets the moment happen.

For five years in Kabul, the iPhone was that camera. And the project it produced is the most important work I have made.
